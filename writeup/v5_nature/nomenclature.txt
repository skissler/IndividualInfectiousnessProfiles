  1. The parameter ψ itself

  Current names in the manuscript: "smoothness parameter," "punctuation parameter." Both are
  descriptive but forgettable.

  Options:
  - Burstiness (ψ → 0 = bursty, ψ → 1 = sustained). This term has precedent in physics and network
  science (Barabási's work on bursty human dynamics). It's immediately evocative, scales naturally
  ("how bursty is transmission?"), and works as an adjective. The downside is that ψ = 1 is the "not
  bursty" end, so you'd either need to define ψ as burstiness (and flip the scale so 1 = maximally
  bursty) or call it something like "sustainedness" for the other end, which is awkward.
  - Concentration (ψ → 0 = concentrated, ψ → 1 = diffuse). Clean, precise, no jargon. "How
  concentrated is an individual's infectiousness in time?" But "concentration" already means other
  things in biology.
  - Sharpness (ψ → 0 = sharp, ψ → 1 = broad). Works visually — you can picture a sharp vs broad
  infectiousness profile. Slightly less evocative than burstiness.
  - Temporal coherence (ψ → 0 = high coherence, ψ → 1 = low coherence). Borrows from optics/signal
  processing. Precise, but probably too technical for a Nature audience.

  My recommendation: burstiness, but with ψ redefined or relabeled so that high values = bursty.
  Alternatively, keep ψ as-is and refer to "1 − ψ" conceptually as the burstiness. Or just introduce
  a new symbol (say, β... no, taken. Maybe φ or ξ) where ξ = 1 − ψ is the burstiness parameter. But
  honestly, I think you can keep ψ as-is and just call it the "burstiness parameter" with the
  convention that low ψ = bursty — readers can handle that, especially with a good figure.

  2. The individual infectiousness profile a_i(τ)

  Current name: "individual infectiousness profile." Accurate but long and not catchy.

  Options:
  - Transmission window — simple, visual, widely understood. "How wide is a person's transmission
  window?"
  - Infectiousness kernel — more technical, common in the modeling literature.
  - Shedding profile — concrete but ties it to a specific mechanism (pathogen shedding), which is
  narrower than the concept.

  My recommendation: transmission window for the broad-audience framing. It works naturally with
  burstiness: "How bursty is a person's transmission window?"

  3. The mechanism (narrow profiles + variable contacts → overdispersion)

  Current framing: "timing-contact interaction," "overdispersion without individual-level
  heterogeneity." Accurate but neither is a name.

  Options:
  - Coincidence superspreading — captures the core idea: superspreading by coincidence of a burst
  with high contacts. Immediately distinguishes it from "trait-based" superspreading.
  - Bad-luck superspreading — vivid, accessible, slightly informal for Nature but would work in the
  text if not the title. Contrasts nicely with "bad-actor" superspreading (identifying risky
  individuals).
  - Timing-driven superspreading — safe, descriptive, but forgettable.
  - Stochastic superspreading — too vague; all superspreading is stochastic in some sense.
  - Non-targetable overdispersion — leads with the policy implication rather than the mechanism.
  Could work as a secondary framing.

  My recommendation: coincidence superspreading as the primary term, with "bad-luck superspreading"
  as an informal gloss used once or twice in the text to make it concrete. The contrast pair would be
   coincidence superspreading vs trait-based superspreading (or individual superspreading).

  4. The overarching framing / title concept

  The current title — "Individual infectiousness timing as a hidden driver of epidemic variability
  and superspreading" — is descriptive. For Nature, you want something that makes a claim.

  Some directions:
  - Lead with the surprising result: "Superspreading without superspreaders" — punchy, paradoxical,
  immediately hooks. Downside: slightly overstates (you're not saying all superspreading is this
  way).
  - Lead with the new axis: "When you transmit matters as much as how much you transmit" — too long
  for a title but captures the Lloyd-Smith parallel perfectly.
  - Lead with the parameter: "The burstiness of infection shapes epidemic dynamics beyond R₀ and k"
  - Shorter options: "Bursty transmission and the origins of superspreading" / "Coincidence
  superspreading from bursty infectiousness"

  I'd lean toward something like "Superspreading without superspreaders: how bursty infectiousness
  drives epidemic variability" — it's provocative, immediately signals what's new, and the subtitle
  grounds it.
